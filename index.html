Frango à Parmigiana or File à Parmigiana, a Brazilian dish, is a type of fried chicken, consisting of a slice of chicken, breaded with wheat flour and eggs (egg white), breast covered with Parmesan cheese, ham and plenty of tomato sauce and condiments such as oregano and cilantro, to taste.

Picanha is a typical Brazilian type of beef cut. The origin of the name Picanha comes from the Italian butchery treatment, namely the Picatta cut. This stick, called picana (in Spanish), had a stinger at the end and was used to sting cattle in the back of their lower back. Over time, this region of the animal came to be called picana and later picanha. Recently, the increase in the variety of cuts of pork, originated from actions promoted by pig farmers, gave rise to a new cut for admirers of this type of meat, which was called pork rump.

Brazilian feijoada is a dish that consists of a stew of black beans with various types of pork and beef. It is served with farofa, white rice, braised cabbage and sliced orange, among other side dishes. It is a popular dish, typical of Brazilian cuisine. Feijoada is given to dishes from Portuguese-speaking countries such as Portugal, Brazil, Angola, Mozambique and Macau, where it is mainly made from a mixture of beans and meat, usually common with rice. The Brazilian version of the delicacy is a probable adaptation of the Portuguese stew. The Portuguese version originates in the north of the country, the first known mention of the "Brazilian feijoada" was in Recife in 1827.
